Why Buy a Chromebook in 2026?

Chromebooks have come a long way from the basic web browsers they were in 2012. Today's Chromebook Plus models run on fast Intel or MediaTek processors, support Android apps from the Google Play Store, and include AI features built into Google Docs and Photos. They boot in under 10 seconds, get software updates automatically for nearly a decade, and start at under $200.

For anyone who primarily works in a browser — students writing papers, remote workers using Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 online, families streaming and browsing — a Chromebook handles it better than a $400 Windows laptop. The operating system is faster, more secure, and requires no antivirus software.

The tradeoff: Chromebooks don't run traditional Windows software. If you need Adobe Photoshop (desktop version), AutoCAD, or specialized Windows-only applications, you need a Windows machine. But for everything else, a Chromebook at $300 beats a $500 Windows laptop in day-to-day speed and simplicity.

What to Look for in a Chromebook

  • RAM (get 8GB minimum): 4GB Chromebooks struggle with multiple tabs. 8GB handles 15–20 open tabs plus Android apps without slowing down.
  • Storage (64GB is tight — aim for 128GB+): ChromeOS itself is small, but Android apps and downloaded files add up. 128GB gives you comfortable headroom.
  • Auto Update Expiration (AUE): Every Chromebook has an expiration date for software updates. Buy one with at least 5 years of updates remaining. Check Google's official AUE list before purchasing.
  • Chromebook Plus badge: Look for the "Chromebook Plus" designation for AI features, faster processors, and better display requirements baked in as minimum specs.

Chromebook vs Windows Laptop — Which Should You Buy?

Buy a Chromebook if: You mainly use Google Docs, Gmail, YouTube, Netflix, or browser-based apps. You want fast boot times, long battery life, and no virus worries. You're buying for a student who doesn't need specialized Windows software. Or you want to spend under $400 and get something that works well.

Buy a Windows laptop if: You need software that only runs on Windows — Adobe Creative Suite, AutoCAD, certain accounting software, or PC gaming. You need to run .exe files or work in enterprise environments that require Windows-specific tools.

The bottom line: For most everyday users, a Chromebook at $300–$400 outperforms a Windows laptop at the same price in speed, security, and battery life. The gap only matters if you have specific software requirements that Chromebooks can't meet.
